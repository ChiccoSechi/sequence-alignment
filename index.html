Dynamic Programming Sequence Alignment

In bioinformatics, sequence alignment arranges two nucleotide sequences, DNA or RNA, to reveal their similarities, allowing substitutions, insertions and deletions. The best alignment is the one that maximizes a chosen scoring scheme.

Dynamic programming (DP) finds that optimum by breaking the problem into simpler subproblems and reusing their results, instead of testing every possible alignment. The two classic alignment algorithms built on it are Needleman-Wunsch (global) and Smith-Waterman (local).

Needleman-Wunsch

Global Alignment

Finds the optimal alignment between two sequences across their entire length. It is a global alignment algorithm, best suited to sequences that are similar overall and of comparable length.

Smith-Waterman

Local Alignment

Finds the optimal alignment between the best-matching subsequences of two sequences. It is a local alignment algorithm, best suited to spotting a shared region inside otherwise different sequences.
