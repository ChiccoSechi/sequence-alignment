Sequence Alignment

Dynamic Programming Sequence Alignment

In bioinformatics, sequence alignment arranges two nucleotide sequences, DNA or RNA, to reveal their similarities, allowing substitutions, insertions and deletions. The best alignment is the one that maximizes a chosen scoring scheme.

Dynamic programming (DP) finds that optimum by breaking the problem into simpler subproblems and reusing their results, instead of testing every possible alignment. The two classic alignment algorithms built on it are Needleman-Wunsch (global) and Smith-Waterman (local).

Needleman-Wunsch

Global Alignment

Finds the optimal alignment between two sequences across their entire length. It is a global alignment algorithm, best suited to sequences that are similar overall and of comparable length.

Smith-Waterman

Local Alignment

Finds the optimal alignment between the best-matching subsequences of two sequences. It is a local alignment algorithm, best suited to spotting a shared region inside otherwise different sequences.

Sequence Alignment using Heuristics

Dynamic programming finds the optimal alignment, but it runs in O(n × m) time and space: every cell of the matrix must be computed and stored. At genome scale, with billions of base pairs and millions of reads, that cost becomes prohibitive.

A heuristic gives up the guarantee of an optimal answer to stay fast and practical at that scale, focusing expensive computation only where it is likely to pay off. FASTA is one of the classic examples for sequence similarity search.

FASTA

Seed and Extend

Searches for regions of similarity between a query and a sequence database. It follows the seed-and-extend strategy: rather than aligning everything, it anchors on short exact matches to spot promising regions, then computes a full local alignment only around the best diagonal it finds. Fast and approximate, instead of exhaustive.
